SEIZURES (6 QUESTIONS - 10.5% OF EXAM)

Core Concepts

Seizure: Sudden, excessive, uncontrolled electrical discharge of neurons in the brain resulting in transient impairment of consciousness, movement, sensation, or memory.

Epilepsy: Recurrent seizures (2+ unprovoked seizures) in a discernible pattern. Does NOT occur with acute illness.

The 3 Phases of a Seizure (CRITICAL - Test This!)

Prodromal Phase (BEFORE)

Child exhibits mood and behavioral changes. An aura may occur (visual changes, dizziness, unusual sensations). Purpose: Warns child so safety measures can be taken.

Ictal Phase (DURING)

When seizure activity begins and lasts until seizure activity ends. Observable motor/sensory manifestations. This is when you perform seizure precautions.

Postictal Phase (AFTER)

Immediately following the seizure. Child may be drowsy, confused, disoriented. Duration: minutes to hours.

Seizure Classification

FOCAL (Partial) SEIZURES - LIMITED TO ONE BRAIN AREA

Simple Focal Complex Focal
Consciousness: NORMAL (alert)
Duration: < 30 seconds
Aura: May occur
Postictal: NO
Manifestations: One limb, sensory changes		 Consciousness: IMPAIRED
Duration: > 60 seconds
Aura: Frequently present
Postictal: YES
Automatisms: Lip smacking, chewing, sucking

GENERALIZED SEIZURES - ENTIRE BRAIN INVOLVED

Tonic-Clonic (Grand Mal) - MOST COMMON

Consciousness: COMPLETELY LOST for 1-2 minutes

Onset: Abrupt

Tonic Stage: Arms flex, legs extend, eyes roll upward, piercing cry

Clonic Stage: Rhythmic jerking, eyes may roll or deviate, possible incontinence

Postictal: YES - can last minutes to hours

Absence (Petit Mal)

Duration: Brief (5-10 seconds)

Manifestation: Sudden interruption of activity, staring, immediate recovery

Note: Often confused with daydreaming or inattentiveness!

Outcome: Usually cease at puberty

Status Epilepticus - MEDICAL EMERGENCY

Definition: Continuous seizure lasting > 30 minutes OR serial seizures without regaining consciousness

Treatment: ABCs • Lorazepam (first-line) • IV access • Oxygen • Check glucose

Risk: Permanent brain injury and DEATH

Key Seizure Medications

Medication Used For Critical Information Side Effects
Dilantin (Phenytoin) Tonic-clonic, complex partial Therapeutic: 10-20 µg/mL
NEVER mix with glucose - flush IV with normal saline only		 Gingival hyperplasia (gums grow), requires dental care
Phenobarbital (Luminal) Tonic-clonic, partial Sometimes given WITH Dilantin Vitamin D/folic acid deficiency, hyperactivity in kids
Valproic Acid (Depakote) Absence seizures Monitor liver enzymes closely Tremors, sedation, elevated liver enzymes
Lorazepam Status epilepticus FIRST-LINE for status epilepticus Respiratory depression if overdosed

Nursing Management During/After Seizure

DURING Seizure: Stay with child • Remain calm • Protect from injury • Remove nearby objects • Ease to floor if standing • Turn on side if possible • DO NOT force anything in mouth • Provide privacy • Keep suction at bedside
AFTER Seizure: Place on side • Check for injuries • Check vitals and respiratory status IMMEDIATELY • Reassure and reorient child • Allow rest • Document characteristics (duration, body parts, loss of consciousness)

Critical Parent Teaching

NEVER abruptly stop medications - Can cause status epilepticus!

Must gradually taper over several weeks if discontinuing

Continue for 2+ years seizure-free AND normal EEG before stopping

Take at same time daily, with meals preferred

Must wear Medical Alert bracelet/ID

Activity restrictions based on seizure type - most activities allowed unless high-risk for injury

Driving: NOT allowed unless seizure-free for specified period

SPINA BIFIDA (6 QUESTIONS - 10.5% OF EXAM)

Definition

Neural Tube Defect: Incomplete closure of vertebrae and neural tube during fetal development (first 4 weeks - before many women know they're pregnant!)

The 3 Types of Spina Bifida

Occulta Meningocele Myelomeningocele
Hidden Type
No cord herniation
Often asymptomatic
Visible skin dimple/hair
Benign		 Meninges Herniate
Meninges only (not cord)
Bulging sac over spine
Better prognosis
Surgical repair needed		 CORD PROTRUDES
Spinal cord in sac
Most severe type
Permanent paralysis BELOW defect
Multiple surgeries

Associated Complications (Can Be Devastating)

  • Arnold Chiari II Malformation: Hindbrain herniates into spinal canal - monitor for increased ICP
  • Hydrocephalus: Develops in 70% of myelomeningocele cases - watch closely!
  • Neurogenic Bladder: Cannot control urination - requires intermittent catheterization
  • Neurogenic Bowel: Cannot control defecation - bowel training with enemas/suppositories
  • Skeletal Deformities: Hip dislocation, kyphosis, scoliosis, clubfoot
  • LATEX ALLERGY: Multiple surgical exposures and catheterizations = latex sensitivity develops!
  • Learning Problems & Seizures: Common but most have average intelligence

Folic Acid Prevention

All women of childbearing age: 0.4 mcg daily

Women with history of NTD baby: 4 mg daily starting ONE MONTH BEFORE planning pregnancy through first trimester

Nursing Management of Myelomeningocele

PRE-OPERATIVE: INFECTION PREVENTION IS #1 PRIORITY

Goal: Prevent rupture/leakage of sac (opens pathway to meningitis!)

Keep sac MOIST: Sterile saline-soaked non-adhesive gauze

Positioning: Prone or supported on side - NEVER on back (pressure on sac!)

Report Immediately: Any seepage of clear fluid (CSF leak!)

Diaper Management: Change frequently, position so urine/feces flow AWAY from sac

Handling: Minimal, gentle handling only

LATEX-FREE ENVIRONMENT IS CRITICAL

Why: Multiple surgical exposures + catheterizations = multiple latex contacts

Actions:

  • All medical staff use non-latex gloves
  • Flag chart clearly: "LATEX ALLERGY"
  • All equipment must be latex-free
  • Teach parents to read ALL product labels
  • Provide Medical Alert ID for child
  • Educate teachers, relatives, babysitters about latex precautions

POST-OPERATIVE CARE

  • Position prone or side-lying (protect surgical site)
  • Monitor for hydrocephalus (watch for signs of increased ICP)
  • Intermittent catheterization for neurogenic bladder
  • Bowel training with timed enemas or suppositories
  • Nutrition: Breastfeeding encouraged, continue normal feeding as able
  • Meticulous skin care to prevent breakdown

Long-Term Management

Children will need lifelong rehabilitation including braces, crutches, physical therapy, occupational therapy, catheterization routines, bowel management programs, and psychosocial support. Outcomes depend on level of lesion and presence of hydrocephalus.

CEREBRAL PALSY (5 QUESTIONS - 8.8% OF EXAM)

Definition

A group of permanent disorders affecting movement, muscle tone, and posture, resulting from a non-progressive lesion in the immature brain (usually before/during birth or in infancy). NON-PROGRESSIVE means it doesn't get worse, but effects evolve with growth.

The 4 Types of Cerebral Palsy

Type Percentage Key Characteristics Clinical Manifestations
Spastic 70-80% Increased muscle tone (hypertonicity) Stiffness, jerky movements, scissor legs, exaggerated reflexes
Dyskinetic (Athetoid) 10-15% Abnormal involuntary movements Writhing, slow worm-like movements, affects face/tongue, speech difficult
Ataxic 5-10% Cerebellar damage Poor balance, unsteady gait, wide-based, tremor with movement
Mixed Most common overall 2+ types combined Variable features from multiple types

Early Warning Signs (Age < 3 Years) - CRITICAL FOR DIAGNOSIS

By 3 months: Poor head control?

By 6 months: Pushing away or arching back? Stiff arms/legs? Not reaching with both hands?

By 8 months: Cannot sit without support? Persistent preference for one side?

By 12 months: Not babbling? Using only one side of body?

Management & Nursing Care

Pharmacologic Management - Botox is Game Changer

Botulinum Toxin (Botox):

  • Injected directly into tight muscles
  • Inhibits acetylcholine → prevents muscle contraction
  • Allows stretching and physical therapy
  • Onset: 24-72 hours
  • Duration: 3-6 months (requires repeated injections)
  • Purpose: Manage spasticity, improve range of motion, reduce pain

Non-Pharmacologic Management

  • Physical Therapy: ROM exercises, stretching, strengthening, gait training
  • Occupational Therapy: Self-care skills, fine motor training, adaptive equipment
  • Speech Therapy: If speech/swallowing affected
  • Adaptive Equipment: Braces, walkers, crutches, wheelchairs as needed
  • Surgical Interventions: Tendon lengthening, muscle releases for contractures

Nursing Priorities - Psychosocial is Just as Important as Physical

Support Self-Esteem: Set realistic goals, PRAISE accomplishments, focus on capabilities not limitations

Encourage Independence: Let child do as much as possible for themselves with adaptive equipment

Manage Complications: Watch for seizures, speech difficulties, vision/hearing loss, dental problems, feeding issues

Nutrition: May have reflux, swallowing difficulties, constipation - careful feeding position, monitor weight

School & Social: IEP coordination, peer relationships, community involvement

HYDROCEPHALUS (5 QUESTIONS - 8.8% OF EXAM)

Definition

Excessive accumulation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) in the brain ventricles, leading to increased intracranial pressure. Think of it as a plumbing problem - either too much fluid is made, it's not being absorbed, or the flow is blocked.

Signs in INFANTS (Fontanels Still Open)

MacEwen Sign ("cracked pot" percussion sound) - Very specific!

RAPIDLY INCREASING head circumference - Measure at occipitofrontal circumference. This falls OFF the growth curve!

Tense, full, bulging fontanels (especially anterior)

Sunsetting eyes (LATE sign - indicates severe ICP)

Poor feeding, irritability, vomiting, lethargy

Signs in OLDER CHILDREN (Closed Fontanels)

FRONTAL HEADACHE (especially morning - awakens from sleep!) - Often relieved by vomiting or sitting upright

Projectile vomiting (indicates increased ICP)

Papilledema (optic disc swelling from ICP)

Ataxia (loss of coordination, unsteady gait)

Behavior/personality changes, irritability, confusion

Changes in school performance

Vision problems (strabismus, diplopia)

Treatment: VP SHUNT (Most Common)

How it works: Ventricular catheter placed in lateral ventricle drains excess CSF through valve system to extracranial compartment (usually peritoneum)

Most common: Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) Shunt

Components: Ventricular catheter, flush pump, unidirectional flow valve, distal catheter

Nursing Management: VP SHUNT

POST-OPERATIVE POSITIONING

  • Position on unoperated side initially
  • Keep flat in bed unless signs of increased ICP
  • If ICP elevated, elevate head 15-30 degrees

MONITOR FOR COMPLICATIONS

Shunt Malfunction Signs: Elevated vitals, poor feeding, vomiting, decreased responsiveness, seizures, headache

Shunt Infection Signs: Fever, local inflammation along shunt tract, vomiting, lethargy, signs of increased ICP

FAMILY EDUCATION

  • Monitor for shunt malfunction and infection signs
  • Head circumference monitoring continues (indicates shunt function)
  • Regular follow-up visits, head scans, abdominal X-rays periodically
  • May need IEP for developmental/learning issues
  • Contact sports generally avoided

MENINGITIS (5 QUESTIONS - 8.8% OF EXAM)

Definition

Inflammation of the meninges (membranes surrounding brain and spinal cord) from a pathogen entering the CNS. This is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Can progress rapidly and cause permanent neurological damage or death.

Three Types

Bacterial Viral/Aseptic Tuberculous
Most serious
Meningococcus, Pneumococcus
Sudden onset, very ill
Life-threatening
CSF cloudy/turbid (pus)		 Less severe
Enteroviruses, Mumps
Usually self-limiting
Complete recovery
CSF clear		 Serious
Tuberculosis bacillus
Insidious onset
Long-term meds
Permanent damage risk

Clinical Manifestations

Classic Triad (not always present!):

1. Fever

2. Headache

3. Neck stiffness (difficulty touching chin to chest)

Other signs: Photophobia (light sensitivity), petechial rash, altered mental status, seizures, high-pitched cry (infants), bulging fontanels (infants)

Definitive Diagnosis

LUMBAR PUNCTURE (Spinal Tap) - Only test that definitively diagnoses meningitis!

CSF analyzed for: Cell count/types, glucose level, protein, Gram stain, culture

Findings in bacterial meningitis: Cloudy/turbid CSF (pus), elevated protein, low glucose

Treatment - AGGRESSIVE & IMMEDIATE

Private room | Droplet precautions × 24 hours after starting antibiotics

IV ANTIBIOTICS IMMEDIATELY × 7-10 days (start BEFORE cultures return!)

Broad-spectrum initially: Ampicillin + aminoglycoside OR third-generation cephalosporin

IV corticosteroids WITH antibiotics (reduces inflammation and complications)

Supportive care: IV fluids, fever management, seizure precautions

Prevention

  • Meningococcal vaccine: Age 11-12 years (routine), booster at 16
  • Haemophilus influenzae (Hib) vaccine: Part of routine childhood immunizations
  • Pneumococcal vaccines: PCV13/PCV15, PPSV23 for certain conditions

Nursing Management

Isolation Precautions: Private room, droplet precautions, strict hand hygiene

Establish baseline assessment: Level of consciousness, pupil response, head circumference (infants), vital signs

Frequent reassessment: Frequency depends on severity (may be every 15 minutes initially)

Antibiotic management: Start IMMEDIATELY, monitor peak/trough if aminoglycosides

Symptom management: Headache relief (dark room, pain meds), fever management, seizure precautions

Complications

10-30% of survivors have neurological sequelae including sensorineural hearing loss (most common - all survivors need hearing test!), vision loss, motor impairment, intellectual impairment, seizure disorder, speech/language problems.

GROWTH HORMONE DEFICIENCY & DWARFISM (5 QUESTIONS - 8.8% OF EXAM)

Growth Hormone Deficiency

Definition & Clinical Manifestations

Definition: Inadequate production or secretion of growth hormone

  • Height below 3rd percentile for age and sex - KEY FINDING!
  • Diminished growth rate (falls off growth curve)
  • Immature or "cherubic" face appearance
  • Delayed puberty
  • Hypoglycemia

Diagnosis

Growth measurements: Plot on growth chart over 6-12 months (shows diminished velocity)

GH stimulation tests: Give stimulating agent (glucagon, insulin, etc.). Peak GH < 7-10 ng/mL in at least TWO tests confirms diagnosis

Also check: Thyroid function, electrolytes, IGF-1, IGFBP-3

Treatment: Synthetic Growth Hormone

Medication: Somatropin (Humatrope, Nutropin, Genotropin, others)

Route: Subcutaneous injection

Frequency: Daily (usually at bedtime)

Dosage: 0.16-0.24 mg/kg/week

Results: Growth most evident in first year, gradually decreases subsequently

Duration: Continue until adult height reached (usually 4-7 years)

Nursing Care

  • Support self-esteem and confidence
  • Teach injection technique to parent and child if old enough
  • Home growth chart - measure monthly, plot progress
  • Medication compliance is critical
  • Evaluate with endocrinologist every 3-4 months

Dwarfism

Two Types

Proportionate Dwarfism Disproportionate Dwarfism
Definition: All body parts small AND proportionate
Cause: GH deficiency, genetic disorders
Appearance: Smaller version of normal person
Treatment: Synthetic GH (if GH deficient)		 Definition: Some parts small, others normal/large
Most common: Achondroplasia (genetic bone disorder)
Features: Short limbs, normal head, flat nose bridge, bowed legs
Adult height: ~4 feet
Treatment: Manage complications surgically

Achondroplasia Features

  • Autosomal dominant genetic disorder
  • Most common form of dwarfism
  • Short arms and legs (especially upper)
  • Short fingers (wide separation between middle and ring - "trident hand")
  • Prominent forehead, flattened bridge of nose
  • Progressive bowing of legs, curved lower back

Complications of Dwarfism

  • Physical: Spinal stenosis, hydrocephalus, sleep apnea, hearing loss, dental problems, joint arthritis, back pain
  • Psychosocial (VERY IMPORTANT!): Stereotyping, teasing, limited opportunities, impact on self-esteem, body image issues

Language Matters!

Acceptable terms: "Dwarf," "little person," "person of short stature"

"Midget" is OFFENSIVE and should NEVER be used

Nursing Considerations

  • Car seats with firm back/neck support for safety
  • Adaptive equipment: footstools, reacher tools, custom furniture
  • Healthy diet and activities (swimming, low-impact sports)
  • NO high-impact sports (football, gymnastics, diving)
  • Psychosocial support - build self-esteem, celebrate capabilities
  • School coordination - IEP as needed

Tuesday Evening: Light review only (30 minutes max) • No new material • Get EXCELLENT SLEEP

Wednesday Morning: Eat protein breakfast • Arrive 10 minutes early • Trust your preparation

During the Exam

  • Timing: 57-60 questions = approximately 45-50 seconds per question
  • Strategy: Read ALL answer choices before selecting • Flag difficult questions and return to them • Easier questions first = confidence building
  • Context clues: Look at patient age + signs presented = points toward diagnosis
  • Nursing priority: SAFETY first, then comfort and education

Memory Hacks for the Test

  • DILANTIN = 'Gums grow big' (gingival hyperplasia - requires dental visits!)
  • HYDROCEPHALUS = 'Cracked pot sign' (MacEwen sign percussion sound)
  • CLUBFOOT = 'Cannot be fixed by hand' (RIGID)
  • GOWER SIGN = 'Child CLIMBS up own legs' (muscular dystrophy weakness)
  • BARLOW TEST = 'SNAP OUT' (adduction, hip leaves socket)
  • ORTOLANI TEST = 'CLUNK IN' (abduction, hip returns to socket)
  • SEIZURE MEDS: Never stop abruptly! Taper gradually over weeks
